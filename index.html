Our team name

In thinking about a name for our team, we drew inspiration from prominent pioneers in information technology. One of our members brought their personal favourite, Grace Hopper, back to the team. Hopper was a real admiral in the US Navy, was one of the first programmers on the Harvard Mark I computer, invented one of the first linkers, and popularised the concept of machine-independent programming languages, leading to the development of COBOL. As an extremely accomplished, entrepreneurial, and forthright person, she is a great figure to draw inspiration from in our activities.

To learn more about Hopper, go to https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Hopper

Changes to our processes for Assignment 3

Overall, we were happy with our processes from Assignment 2, they functioned well, and most members had little issues with the tools or techniques we employed. Despite this, we made several adjustments to our processes, both from the onset and throughout the development of Assignment 3, further refining our processes, and ensuring delivery of the project was as smooth and enjoyable as possible for all.

As Assignment 3 had a less defined mandate than Assignment 2, additional upfront planning and collaboration was required. We used online collaborative tooling extensively to overcome our geographical distances and conflicting schedules.

Miro is one of the new tools we employed; it is a collaborative diagrammatic tool we used to complete our initial planning. Miro allowed team members to contribute to the board at any time that was convenient to them, allowing ideas to gradually form over the opening weeks. The Miro app and our project board is below, it shows some of the ideas, clusters, and work breakdowns we did as part of the assignment:

Due to our tooling experience acquired in Assignment 2, the team had a good understanding of how our tools worked, allowing us to build on our knowledge and practices. We continued to rely on Slack, Jira and Zoom for communications and work delegation, however, we added to the frequency we used these tools.

The team felt that Assignment 2 read like a collection of different voices, rather than one consistent piece, thus we agreed we needed more specific requirements in our Jira stories to help adopt more consistent tone. Our Jira items had greater detail in them than Assignment 2, and we employed an unwritten agreement of a “definition-of-done” between members for Assignment 3, including spelling, grammar, and tone we were expecting from our teammates. An example of our Jira’s item content is below:

Lastly, we created a new website, GitHub repository, and build pipeline for Assignment 3. Feedback from Assignment 2’s reviewer suggested we use pull-requests to gate commits before merging, in a software development project, pull-requests are an essential part of the development process, however as a team we decided against this, we considered gating an unnecessary level of bureaucracy for a largely non-technical project. We considered our site as the HTML equivalent of a collaborative Google Doc, thus, as the team were committing non-breaking changes to the site, there was little risk of problems arising.
