Topic

The World Economic Forum lists five major challenges for big cities of the future on their website. Our project idea addresses three out of the five while it will also have an impact on the remaining two eventually. The challenges identified by the World Economic Forum are Environmental Threats, Resources, Inequality, Technology and Governance. Our project idea is a value proposition that can help big cities transform their transport networks for a sustainable future. (Chee, 2019)

The idea is to develop a mobile app, combining a loyalty program (like FlyBuys or Velocity) with a mobility planning tool, incentivising the use of public modes of transportation, or the use of toll roads outside of peak hour traffic. The app usage by commuters is seeking to ease traffic congestion around major arteries of Australian capital cities. Therefore, the App requires an integrated approach; tapping into third party applications like Toll Road, Public Transport or Parking Apps to facilitate commuters into desired forms of transportation. The five challenges the World Economic Forum has identified are all based on one underlying trend which is the urbanisation in modern industrialised countries. Urbanisation is defined as an increase in population in cities and towns rather than rural areas. Infrastructure Australia, an independent statutory body for research and advice on infrastructure needs, claims that Australia’s cities are even facing a watershed moment regarding their growth trend and development. Between 2017 and 2046 Australia’s population is projected to grow by 11.8 million people. And up to 75% of this increase in population with occur in Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane and Perth. (Infrastructure Australia, 2018)

The opportunities for cities are big but so are the risks. Environmental Threats by a growing number of vehicles on the roads for example. Resources will come under scrutiny and electricity grids might reach their max capacity. Lastly, Technology can help cities adapt, however, to master the right applications can be quite challenging. This is where our project idea comes into play. A technological app solution to manage resources more efficiently in order to avoid environmental threats and educate a new generation of commuters.

Motivation

The motivation for this project stems from personal experience but also from seeing the bigger picture in a world that is facing climate change and its implications all around the world. Cities like Melbourne or Sydney are experiencing incredible population growth numbers and therefore an ever increasing amount of vehicles on major arteries. In a group discussion at the start of this assignment we all realised that being stuck in traffic is a situation we are all too familiar with. Furthermore, we were all realising that with the current transportation network and the increasing number of vehicles on our Australian roads, cities would eventually collapse under the demands of commuters with a catastrophic result for the environment and the living conditions in major urban centres. A solution addressing this situation and allowing subsequent generations to thrive is a challenge, but with one of the major IT trends being “ Advanced Networking” on the backbone of the introduction of 5G technology and new IoT standards as well as the rise of smart cities we think our project is able to succeed. (Deloitte Australia, 2019)

As private as well as public modes of transportation on macro or micro level become more interconnected, the only thing we have had to add to the equation was the rewards program in order to incentivise smarter transport choices and therefore educate society. In order to target the daily commuter in the most efficient way, the only realistic option was the development of an app to bring our services together and provide a smarter digital touchpoint. In our view, this project has incredible potential and with the right partners could even be realised in the long run. Future employers would be able to recognise the complexity of the project and realise that only with precise planning, open communication and the right IT skill sets combined in our team this endeavour is manageable. In the end, this project might even attract one of the major 5G network companies to assist in order to gain public reputation as a sustainable and innovative market player.

Landscape

The market in which our app is positioned is a subset of different segments that are already existing in, in some instances, quite matured markets. The first segment are ride-hailing platforms like Uber and Lift, which have already established a rather large customer base. Companies that offer transport services also include public transport alternatives like Myki for example or can relate to Micro transportation or the so-called last mile service like Lime scooters.

A second segment that has to be monitored is the MaaS area, however, at this stage only small initiatives have developed. As an example, the RACV has launched the multi-modal transportation platform Arevo, but is yet to confirm results as to the uptake of the service. For MaaS, current research projects suggest that it is still undecided who is able to offer a fully integrated transportation platform. Governments as well as private companies are still holding back due to the complexity of the topic. The MaaS segment is likely to be the closest to our anticipated service offering and in the graphic below we indicated our USP vs. other MaaS offerings. The integration of a rewards program is a first within this industry. (ITS Australia, 2018)

Following MaaS, the third potential competitors segment for our App is the customer loyalty segment with established programs like FlyBuys, Velocity or Qantas. This segment is new to the initiatives in the smart mobility sector, however it could revolutionise the uptake and education of modern commuters by incentivising different trip options. The fourth segment is the automotive sector and also road toll companies as they enable the private and self-controlled transport as well as have access to important traffic management data. Lastly, the big technology companies like Google and Apple with their map applications offer today’s transport intelligence for the largest group of users via in-vehicle systems.

To conclude, there are several market segments that offer similar services and competitors can arise in either of them. However, the USP with the integration of a rewards program to educate daily commuters to change there habits is what sets our app apart from all other alternatives. A partnership with e.g. the RACV and their multi-modal platform for mobility as a service may be considered. In order to get a better understanding of our app service solution including our USP, we have also identified different strength and weaknesses in a detailed SWOT Analysis which is presented below.

Aims

Transport is Australia’s third largest source of greenhouse gas emissions, with emissions from transport increasing nearly 60% since 1990, more than any other sector. [1]

According to a recent update, published by the Australian Government, Australia produced 535.7 million tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (Mt CO2-e) emissions, almost a fifth of these emissions are caused by transport (17%). Cars are responsible for roughly half of all transport emissions. [2] Car emissions have grown by 25% since 1990. [3]

To reduce these climate polluting emissions, we promote greener commuting through public transport, Linkt and electric cars.

Thus, our project’s aim is to create an application that helps slow down Global Warming by reducing the emission of greenhouse gases through car emissions.

Goals

Our aim inspired many robust discussions amongst the group, and from these conversations we generated a list of goals that would drive our aim. These initial goals were seemingly disparate at first, however, as we refined our vision, we were able to cluster these goals into three main objectives driving the initial stages of the project, these include:

To create attention for the Smatra programme and its app, establish a brand strategy

For Smartra to be a success, it needs a clearly identifiable brand, messaging, role, and media presence. From this work, we need to identify the most appropriate target markets, prove out potential market messaging, including; product, advertising, positioning, value propositions, etc. This will help ensure our user base is engaged and growing, that we can continually drive our app usage, and eventually transition from completely artificial growth to a natural growth of our user base.

To establish an attractive rewards programme that promotes the usage of transport alternatives, build partnerships with transport and rewards vendors

Our programme will require partnerships with third-party vendors, both in transport and rewards programme spaces. Transportation partnerships will be required to track trips on transportation alternatives, encouraging trips at reduced congestion times across varying forms of transport.

From trips taken, end users will acquire points. Partnerships will be required with points programme vendors to convert our points to theirs, allowing our end users to enjoy rewards from their trips.

Build an app that provides value, with clear journeys through the application, ultimately driving its use

We require an application that is easy to use, encourages trips at less congested times, and guides the user through the process of trips, to points, to rewards efficiently. This application will need to be secure, reliable, and beneficial to the end user, encouraging its reuse and end user advocacy.

Testing

To successfully test Smatra, we need to define quantifiable measures against our projects goals to measure our success. Below is a list of our goals and how we intend to test them in a quantifiable way:

  • Establish a brand strategy for Smatra to create attention for the App amongst younger target groups
    1. Through A/B testing
  • Establish an attractive rewards programme that promotes smarter travel by building partnerships with vendors and providing a joint venture with their own store rewards programs

  • Have the app provide value to the end user and have the value proposition clear and communicated
    1. Through regular, consistent usage of the app in the way of points accrual and usage, ensure that points are producing desirable outcomes for both commuters and transport vendors

Risks

During the planning of SMATRA we identified a number of risks with the project our aim with the this is to be able to effectively manage and or mitigate these issues so as they do not cause any long-term ill effects to the project. With software development projects in general there are five key areas of impact, we have divided our identified risks into the following areas.

New technologies
There is significant risk of not currently having the skill sets available within the existing team to fully develop and deploy the Smartra application. Training and upskilling on latest developments in networking and mobile phone technology will require time, onboarding skilled experts or advisors in areas to fill skill gaps may be required.
Recommendations
Charting the skills that are required for project completion and mapping our team’s skills against this chart will help us to identify any skill gaps that will need to be filled.

User and functional requirements
There is a substantial risk of scope creep and the need for extensive refactoring during the project due to poor planning of the application requirements.
Recommendations
Before beginning the programming of the SMARTRA application we need a clear and concise plan of what features will be available to the end user. A top down programming approach will deliver a structure and allow us to mitigate the need for extensive refactoring during the development lifecycle. Properly structuring the program from the beginning will make the inclusion of new features a lot less painful when the need for them is discovered during development.

Application and system architecture
The wrong choice of platform for the development of the application could become a critical problem in the later phases if the architecture requirements are not supported in the framework chosen by the team.
Recommendations
A detailed analysis of the architecture requirements for the application either by the current team or this work could be outsourced to a skilled software engineer to analyse the applications architectural requirements.

Performance
There is a chance that the application will not meet the expectations of our internal and external customers. In adequate testing in the development lifecycle may result in the development of a buggy sub-standard application that falls well short of expectations.
Recommendations
A clear benchmark for the requirements of the application and well defined and documented testing procedures will need to be developed.

Organizational
Not having a well-structured team with clearly defined goals is a danger to any project, members that are not focused on the delivery of a world class product can affect the entire team and degrade the overall project outcomes.
Recommendations
In the early stages of the project it may be worthwhile to do some team building exercises to help solidify the team. Weekly discussions on not just how the work is going but how the team members are handling as many factors outside of work can affect human performance.

Conclusion
Our aim is not to avoid risks completely with this project as all software development endeavours are inherently risky. Our aim is to understand the risks and find ways to manage them reducing their potential to have a damaging impact on the project

Group Process and Communications

Meetings have moved from one long Monday night meeting to shorter, more frequent meetings, and due to the correspondence over Slack between Zoom sessions, allowed for much more constructive professional conversations when a Zoom session was hosted. More frequent meetings meant that there were more opportunities for those who missed our Monday night meetings to join another night, and if a team member missed a session, they were able to catch the minutes on Slack later. Over time, especially if the project was to become commercial, the team would likely benefit from adopting Agile development practices; where daily stand-ups, retrospectives and kick-offs provide structure to this frequent level of communication.
